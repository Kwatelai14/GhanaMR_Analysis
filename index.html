Project Overview

Understanding Ghana's Fight Against Vaccine-Preventable Diseases

This project analyses 14 years of measles-rubella surveillance data across Ghana's 10 administrative regions. The analysis tracks vaccination coverage, disease burden reduction, and persistent equity gaps between northern and southern Ghana, informing targeted public health interventions.

Key Findings

What the Data Reveals

📈 Dramatic National Progress

Ghana achieved a 92.9% reduction in measles-rubella cases between 2010 and 2023, with national vaccination coverage rising from 68.3% to 89.4% — one of the strongest trajectories in West Africa.

🎯 Southern Regions Near Target

Greater Accra (96%) and Ashanti (95%) have met the WHO 95% elimination threshold. Eastern, Central, Western, and Brong-Ahafo are within 5 percentage points and expected to reach target by 2026.

⚖️ Persistent North–South Equity Gap

Northern, Upper East, and Upper West regions lag southern counterparts by 10.6 percentage points. Despite gains, these three regions remain below the 85% threshold needed for herd immunity.

📊 Strong Statistical Correlation

Pearson correlation of r = −0.78 (p < 0.001) confirms a highly significant inverse relationship between vaccination coverage and disease burden — causal evidence supporting programme scale-up.

⚠️ Acceleration Needed in North

At the current rate of improvement, Upper West will not reach the 95% WHO target until 2034. Accelerated campaigns, mobile vaccination units, and community health worker deployment are recommended.

💊 Programme Efficiency Improving

Cases eliminated per thousand doses administered increased year-on-year — indicating improved targeting, cold chain management, and outreach strategy across the programme lifetime.
